I. The Show

What It Is

A serialized animated drama set in a near-future coastal city that has perfected the art of looking good.

Westgate follows two estranged sisters — Candy and Aadya — who inherit their father's experimental exosuit technology and stumble into a conspiracy that the city's gleaming surface was built to hide.

Season One is a slow burn. The mutations, the cult, the government — but the deeper story is about two women learning that the world they grew up believing in was partly a lie, and figuring out who they are once the lie stops working.

When the world you were raised in turns out to have been built on someone else's suffering — what do you do with the person you became inside it?

The Feel

Episodic early, serialized by the end. The first three episodes feel like a contained genre show: strange incidents, two people with remarkable technology, a beautiful city. By episode six, the genre scaffolding starts coming down. By the finale, it's gone entirely.

Visually: the city proper is whites and golds, clinical and aspirational. The lower districts are color and chaos, live music coming from doorways. The contrast should feel like walking out of an air-conditioned building during summer.

Reference: the slow revelation of Arcane, the grounded action of Into the Badlands — but the emotional texture is very coming-of-age and familial.

II. The World

Westgate

A coastal city in the near future. The city climbs up from the water with towers of white and gold, vertical gardens, magrail lines that run on time, and clean air. The coast is expensive. The further inland you go, the more the city stops performing.

Westgate is genuinely good for the people who live inside it. That's what makes it complicated. The healthcare works, the streets are safe, and the technology is remarkable. The lie isn't that the city is secretly terrible. The lie is that the city doesn't know what it costs.

The City Proper

White stone and glass, holographic signage, air that doesn't require a respirator. People here are comfortable, and most of them are not cruel. They just don't look inland.

The Lower Districts

Inland. The city's original bones. The air requires medication or masks. Information is slower here. But there's something the city has lost — community, street art nobody commissioned, live music from doorways. A culture that grows itself rather than being curated. This is what's burning when the mutations come. The audience needs to have seen it before it's threatened.

The Magrail

The city's transit system is elevated, fast, and beautiful. It does not extend to the lower districts in any useful way. This is not an accident.

The Mutations

A street drug — or something moving through street drug supply chains — is causing unpredictable, violent physical transformations. They are horrifying. They are also, undeniably, the result of something being done to people, not something happening randomly. The mutations are not the show's monster. The people who mutate were people first.

The Frame

Two experimental exosuit prototypes left in a storage unit by the sisters' father. Modular. High-spec. The neural link doesn't just share movement data — intentions, resistances, and emotions bleed through. Aadya and Candy can feel when the other one doesn't mean it.

III. Characters

The Cast

Candy
"She's been looking for something real her whole life — she just hasn't learned yet to check whether it's safe."
An artist. Expressive, professionally unstable in the way creatives in expensive cities often are. She leads with her heart because she's found it a more reliable instrument than the alternatives. She idealized their father. In the suits, she improvises — reckless and often right. She hears the person inside the monster.
Aadya
"She put her life on hold for a sister she was already estranged from, because of a father who left them a box of secrets."
Older. Institutional. Methodical, capable, quietly exhausted. Her arc is not discovering her city is secretly terrible — it's discovering the city she loves is built on a foundation she didn't consent to and didn't know to question. In the suits, she wants protocols.
Elias
"He's right about what Westgate is. He's wrong about what that means he's allowed to do."
Not from Westgate. Came from somewhere distant — a community extracted and abandoned. He is warm. He listens like it's the most important thing anyone has said to him. He tears the roof off to let the light in and doesn't care that the people inside are now freezing.
Willow
"She'd have to grieve her brother before she could leave him."
Survived the same childhood as Elias. The cult's scalpel — fast, precise. She mirrors Candy in raw power and loyalty, but where Candy's loyalty is still searching for its object, Willow's has already been claimed. She has no identity left outside of Elias's cause. She is what Candy could become.
Agent Thorne
"The exhausted company man with a conscience."
He knows what the system he works for actually is and has decided the alternative — genuine collapse — is worse. He knew the sisters' father. He is not their enemy. He is also not entirely their ally. His affection for Aadya and Candy is genuine and strange and completely at odds with his professional obligations.
Their Father
"He left them a storage unit and no explanation."
A visionary who made a compromise. He saw what was coming and tried to engineer a solution. The people funding his work stripped the solution out and kept the weapon. He gave his daughters a comfortable, high-society childhood, knowing he was building the thing that made it possible. He is not a villain. He is something harder to deal with than that.

He is alive. Thorne knows where he is.
IV. The Conspiracy

The government is not orchestrating Elias. They are managing him and keeping his operation at a size and temperature that is useful. As long as Elias is scary, the citizens of the city will give the government whatever authority it asks for. The mutations justify the surveillance. The cult justifies the crackdowns.

Elias believes he is the revolution. He does not know he is a controlled burn.

The father's research sits at the center of all of it. The Frame and the serum were originally one project — two halves of an attempt to help people survive in a city whose environment was quietly becoming toxic. The government split the project. Kept the serum. Gave the suits to a man they thought they could control.

When and if Elias finds out he's been managed — that is a different show, in a Season Two.

V. Season One

Episode Outline

The Shape
EPS 1–3
The lie is comfortable. Westgate is beautiful. Something is too clean.
EPS 4–6
The cracks. The lower districts. The father's shadow grows.
EPS 7–10
Everything the audience suspected begins to be confirmed. Candy is the last to see it.
1
Episode One"Westgate"

The pilot's job is to make you want to live here. We spend real time in the city before anything goes wrong — the magrail at golden hour, the high-rise markets, the way people dress, talk, and move.

  • They find the storage unit. Their father left them two Frame prototypes and nothing else.
  • A mutation event happens before they understand what they're holding. A magrail worker transforms. They suit up out of desperation.
  • The worker collapses back into something almost human. They stand in borrowed armor, shaking.
  • Thorne appears at the edge of the scene. Watches, then walks away.
Build: Love for Westgate · Curiosity about the father · Suits as weight, not power fantasy
2
Episode Two"The High Life"

Life returns to normal, or at least tries to. A second mutation at a nightclub — a performer bursts into flames. They respond. Better this time, but still fractured.

  • Candy hears the person inside the mutation when they scream out. Something shifts in her.
  • Afterward, Candy meets Elias. Warm, perceptive, interested in a way that feels genuine. The audience notices: he asks more questions than he answers.
Build: The friendship begins · First seed of doubt · Fracture point quietly established
3
Episode Three"Below the Line"

A lead pulls them into the lower districts for the first time. The tonal shift is immediate — loud, colorful, real in a way the High Zone isn't.

  • They are clumsy down here. People don't trust them or the tech they're wearing.
  • Candy tells Elias about the lower districts. He listens like it's the most important thing anyone has said to him.
  • Willow is seen twice. Background. Watching.
Build: Westgate's underside established · Dramatic irony around Elias activated · Willow as presence before character
4
Episode Four"Dead Weight"

Aadya finds something in the Frame's firmware. Old logs, partial and corrupted. Enough to know the suits weren't a hobby project. Enough to know her father was in contact with someone in the government.

  • A bridge collapses. A mutated structural worker. They fight without a key module that's been malfunctioning.
  • Thorne makes proper contact. No threats. He tells Aadya her father was careful, and it didn't save him.
Build: The father as mystery, not myth · Thorne's protectiveness · Aadya's certainty begins to erode
5
Episode Five"Elara"

The sisters realize they've been fed bad information. Someone has been redirecting them, mapping their patterns, manipulating their leads for weeks.

  • Elara ("Whisper"), the cult's spy, becomes visible without fully revealing herself.
  • A quiet scene between Candy and Elias. No mutations. He takes her to a community garden. He talks about the city like he loves it and grieves it simultaneously.
  • The episode ends on a shot Candy won't notice: Elara in the background of the garden. She knew where Candy was because Elias told her.
Build: Dramatic irony fully activated · The betrayal needs to be expensive
6
Episode Six"The Factory"

The season's first genuinely large-scale event. Contaminated water, dozens of people mutating at once. They save people, but not everyone.

  • For the first time, they are triaging — not containing an incident.
  • They find evidence: the contamination was deliberate. The vials were introduced intentionally.
  • Aadya wants to take what they found to Thorne. Candy hesitates. They fight — genuinely, bitterly.
  • Final shot: Elias watching news coverage of the factory. His expression is unreadable.
Build: The conspiracy moves from abstract to concrete · The sisters' fracture deepens · Suspicion of Elias crystallizes
7
Episode Seven"Willow"

Willow steps out of the background. The confrontation is a hunt, not a setpiece. She is faster and more precise than anything they've faced.

  • She doesn't try to kill them. She delivers a message: their father said the suits would protect them. Ask Thorne what they were really designed for.
  • Aadya goes to Thorne. He doesn't deny it.
  • Candy doesn't know about this conversation yet. She is with Elias.
Build: Willow as a character, not just a threat · The father's compromise surfaces · Sisters in separate orbits
8
Episode Eight"Mara"

Mara, the mother of the cult, appears in the lower districts — not as a monster, but as someone people actually trust. She runs something between a shelter and a community.

  • Some of the people here have mutated and stayed. They are living. Differently, awkwardly, in bodies that don't match the city they were born in.
  • Mara speaks to Candy alone and plants a question: your father built those suits to contain people. Did he tell you that?
  • Meanwhile, Aadya is assembling the shape of something larger. She is scared.
Build: Moral complexity around the mutations · The sisters' worldviews are actively diverging · Mara as counterweight
9
Episode Nine"Signal"

Coordinated mutation events across multiple districts simultaneously. The sisters fight in sync for the first time — genuinely. Under the worst possible pressure. It matters because we've watched them fail to sync for eight episodes.

  • After: Thorne tells them what he knows. Their father is alive. He vanished because he found out what the government planned. Thorne has been sitting on this.
  • Candy goes to Elias. He is gentle and present and says almost nothing — just lets her talk. The audience sees his eyes while she talks. He is not her friend.
Build: Emotional peak before the collapse · The father reveal landing hard · Elias's mask slips for the audience, not for Candy
10
Episode Ten"The Beautiful Lie"

Elias moves openly. A city-wide address. He is articulate and right about most of what he says — which is what makes him dangerous.

  • Candy gets to him first, alone. The scene between them doesn't have a speech. He doesn't explain himself. He looks at her like she was always going to end up on the wrong side of him, and he is almost sad about it.
  • The sisters stand together in the lower districts, suits battered, skyline altered behind them. They don't know what they are now.
  • Willow: in the crowd around Elias, the true believers are cheering. The camera finds her face. She is not cheering.
  • No resolution. No score. Let it sit.
Build: Everything planted across the season pays · Season Two earns its premise
VI. The Core

What This Show Is About

The mutations are not the enemy. The cult is not the enemy. The government is not the enemy — or not just the enemy. The enemy is the comfortable story a city tells about itself when it doesn't want to look at what the story is costing.

Elias is dangerous because he's right about the story. He's wrong about what it gives him the right to do.

Aadya and Candy are the show's moral center not because they have the answers but because they keep showing up — imperfectly, angrily, together and apart — to do something in a situation where every clean option has already been used up.

The sisters don't arrive at a clean moral position by the end of Season One. They arrive at a harder place: aware, uncertain, unable to go back, not yet sure how to go forward.
